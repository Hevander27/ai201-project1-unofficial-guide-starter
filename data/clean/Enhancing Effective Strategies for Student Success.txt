Abstract
This perspective highlights three often overlooked but highly effective methods for boosting student performance
in medical education. Active recall, which involves retrieving information from memory during learning, helps
strengthen understanding and retention of complex medical concepts. Spaced repetition, spreading out study sessions
over time, enhances long-term memory formation and reduces forgetting. Both techniques have been shown to
outperform traditional passive review and cramming. We have also emphasized the importance of lifestyle factors,
particularly sleep and diet, in academic success. Studies using wearable devices have linked better sleep quality to
higher grades, with poor sleep habits significantly hurting performance. To implement these approaches, medical
schools should offer training sessions on evidence-based learning strategies for students and faculty. Creating an
environment that supports active learning and healthy habits can increase student engagement, even in the demanding
field of medicine. By adopting these methods, medical students can develop a deeper grasp of their studies and become
more capable practitioners. The perspective argues for a deeper understanding of scientific material to improving
medical education, combining effective study techniques with lifestyle management to produce well-prepared future
doctors.

1. Active recall
Current studies indicate that these strategies are
more effective and efficient for learning than the
conventional ones [1]. Active recall, which involves
retrieving information from the brain throughout
the learning process, and spaced repetition, which
includes the distribution of review sessions over
time, have been scientifically shown to activate
several brain regions, improving learning and
memory [2]. Active recall is a powerful learning
technique where students deliberately retrieve in-
formation from memory during the study process.
Unlike passive reading, this method requires med-
ical students to actively reconstruct knowledge,
strengthening neural connections and improving
long-term retention. Research demonstrates that
actively testing oneself on medical concepts leads to
deeper understanding and more efficient informa-
tion processing [3,4]. By consistently challenging
their memory, students can develop stronger
cognitive pathways, enabling faster and more
comprehensive comprehension of complex medical
information [3,4] (Table 1). A report emphasizes that
students who follow these tactics perform better [2].
Such tactics create more trained and capable med-
ical practitioners. Medical students can implement
active recall through various methods, such as
creating flashcards, participating in study groups, or
explaining concepts to peers.
Cognitive learning theory examines how the
human brain processes and retains information,
offering valuable insights for medical education.
This approach draws parallels between human
cognition and computer data processing, high-
lighting the need for effective information acquisi-
tion and storage. In the context of medical training,
cognitive theory underpins innovative teaching
methods like problem-based learning and organ-
system curricula. These approaches aim to foster
critical thinking and clinical reasoning abilities,
which are essential for future healthcare
professionals.

education helps students develop robust mental
frameworks for organizing complex medical
knowledge. By understanding how the brain en-
